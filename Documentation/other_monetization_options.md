# Monetization Options

## 1.0 Purpose

This document lists practical monetization options for this project and the main pros and cons of each.

## 1.1 One-Time Paid App

### 1.1.1 Pros

- Simple offer users understand quickly.
- Fits a fully local app with no backend costs.
- No recurring billing support needed.

### 1.1.2 Cons

- Revenue is one-off per customer.
- Requires steady new sales for stable income.
- Price set too low can make support unsustainable.

## 1.2 Free Or Lite App With One-Time Pro Unlock

### 1.2.1 Pros

- Easier user acquisition through free entry point.
- Strong conversion path to paid features.
- Still avoids subscription complexity.

### 1.2.2 Cons

- More product complexity due to feature gating.
- Requires careful free versus paid boundary.
- Can increase support questions about limits.

## 1.3 One-Time Paid App With Paid Major Upgrades

### 1.3.1 Pros

- Creates repeat revenue without subscriptions.
- Matches offline software buyer expectations.
- Encourages meaningful version milestones.

### 1.3.2 Cons

- Upgrade value must be clear each cycle.
- Existing users may resist paying again.
- Version support policy must be managed.

## 1.4 One-Time Paid App With One-Time Add-On Packs

### 1.4.1 Pros

- Adds upsell opportunities without recurring billing.
- Lets users pay only for features they need.
- Works well for templates and advanced modules.

### 1.4.2 Cons

- Adds packaging and entitlement complexity.
- Can fragment user experience across tiers.
- Requires ongoing add-on roadmap to grow revenue.

## 1.5 Pay-What-You-Want Pricing

### 1.5.1 Pros

- Low barrier can increase adoption.
- Some users voluntarily pay more.
- Good fit for community-first positioning.

### 1.5.2 Cons

- Revenue is unpredictable.
- Many users choose the minimum amount.
- Hard to forecast business sustainability.

## 1.6 Open Source Core With Paid Packaged Builds

### 1.6.1 Pros

- Keeps code open while monetizing convenience.
- Reduces friction for non-technical customers.
- Can bundle premium support and setup content.

### 1.6.2 Cons

- Open source users can self-build for free.
- Value proposition must be convenience and trust.
- Requires release automation discipline.

## 1.7 Open Source With Donations Or Sponsorships

### 1.7.1 Pros

- Very simple to launch and maintain.
- Keeps product fully open and community friendly.
- Good supplemental funding channel.

### 1.7.2 Cons

- Usually low and inconsistent revenue.
- Difficult to depend on as primary income.
- Funding may not scale with support burden.

## 1.8 Dual License For Personal And Commercial Use

### 1.8.1 Pros

- Maintains free personal adoption.
- Generates B2B revenue from commercial users.
- Common and proven open-core legal model.

### 1.8.2 Cons

- Licensing terms must be clear and enforceable.
- Can create confusion without simple messaging.
- May require legal review and policy upkeep.

## 1.9 Sell Entire Codebase Or License Outright

### 1.9.1 Pros

- Fast one-time cash outcome.
- Minimal long-term maintenance obligation.
- Useful if builder wants to exit fully.

### 1.9.2 Cons

- No long-term upside after sale.
- Buyer due diligence can take time.
- Product direction control is surrendered.

## 1.10 White-Label Licensing

### 1.10.1 Pros

- Higher-value B2B deals are possible.
- One product can serve multiple client brands.
- Can include paid customization packages.

### 1.10.2 Cons

- Client customization requests can expand scope.
- Support and contract complexity increases.
- Requires clearer product boundaries and SLAs.

## 1.11 Practical Fit For Current Architecture

- Best fit for current local-only architecture: one-time paid app, paid major upgrades, and one-time add-on packs.
- Less natural fit without backend: recurring subscriptions, unless they include ongoing content or support value.
