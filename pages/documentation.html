Getting Started: Create Your First Budget

A budget is your financial roadmap. It helps you plan your income and expenses so you can see if your money will last through the month, and identify where you can save. FTrack makes this simple by letting you forecast multiple scenarios and compare them side-by-side.

What is a Scenario?

A scenario is a financial plan. You might have a baseline scenario showing your current reality, plus scenarios for "if I save $200/month" or "if I get a raise." Each scenario contains the same accounts, but with different transactions and projections.

Step-by-Step: Build Your First Budget

  1. Create a scenario: Click the Forecast button, then create a new scenario named "My Budget" and set the type to Budget.
  2. Add your accounts: List the accounts where your money lives (e.g., Checking, Savings, Credit Card). Enter each account's current balance.
  3. Add your income: Create a Planned Transaction for your paycheck. Set it to recur weekly or monthly depending on your pay schedule.
  4. Add your expenses: Add Planned Transactions for recurring expenses like rent, utilities, groceries, and subscriptions. Use recurrence for monthly or weekly bills.
  5. Configure recurrence: For repeating payments, set them to recur on the correct day each week or month.
  6. Run a projection: Click "Generate Projections" to see your forecasted balances over the next 90 days.
  7. Review and adjust: Look at the projection. If your balance goes negative, adjust your spending or add more income until it looks sustainable.
  8. Save your plan: Your budget is automatically saved. You can now track it and compare it to actual spending.

Pro Tips for Beginners

  • Start simple: Don't worry about every small expense. Focus on the big items first (rent, paycheck, major bills).
  • Keep one baseline: Always have one scenario that matches reality. Use it to compare "what-if" scenarios against.
  • Recurrence is your friend: Most expenses are monthly or weekly. Use recurrence so you don't have to enter each payment manually.
  • Log actuals regularly: Once a week, mark transactions as "actual" to see how your real spending compares to your plan.
  • Adjust as needed: Your budget isn't set in stone. Update it when your situation changes (raise, new expense, etc.).

Creating an Account

Accounts are the foundation of your financial model. Each account represents a place where money lives, such as a checking account, savings account, credit card, or loan.

  1. Navigate to the Accounts tab within a scenario.
  2. Click the "Add Account" button.
  3. Enter the account name (e.g., "Checking Account").
  4. Select the account type: Asset, Liability, Income, or Expense.
  5. Enter the opening balance (the current balance as of today).
  6. Mark as active or inactive.
  7. Save your account.

Account Types

Asset
Money you have or own (checking, savings, investment accounts).
Liability
Money you owe (credit cards, loans, mortgages).
Income
Money coming in (salary, bonuses, interest earned).
Expense
Money going out (rent, utilities, groceries, subscriptions).

Managing Account Balances

Your opening balance is the starting point for all projections. Keep this synchronized with your real accounts to ensure accurate forecasts.

  • Update opening balances weekly to stay in sync with reality.
  • Use projections to identify when you might run low on funds.
  • Compare projected vs. actual balances to refine your planning.

Transactions Guide

Planned Transactions

  • Purpose: Define all expected future income and expenses.
  • Recurrence: Set weekly, monthly, or custom repeating schedules.
  • Periodic change: Apply increases, decreases, or compounding effects.
  • Dual-entry: Always assign a secondary account for accuracy.

Creating a Planned Transaction

Planned transactions are the backbone of your budget. They represent income and expenses you expect to occur.

  1. Navigate to the Planned Transactions tab.
  2. Click "Add Transaction".
  3. Enter the description (e.g., "Monthly Rent").
  4. Select the primary account (where money comes from or goes to).
  5. Select the secondary account (the other side of the transaction).
  6. Enter the amount.
  7. Set the transaction date.
  8. Configure recurrence if this is a repeating transaction.
  9. Save the transaction.

Actual Transactions

  • Reality check: Mark planned items as completed or adjust amounts.
  • Period filters: Focus on a specific month or time range.
  • Variance: Compare planned vs actual spending and income.

Recording Actual Transactions

As transactions occur in the real world, log them as Actual Transactions. This helps you track how your real spending compares to your plan.

  • Log actual transactions weekly to keep your data current.
  • Compare actuals to planned amounts to identify spending patterns.
  • Use variance analysis to refine future budgets.

Transaction Types

Transactions are categorized by their nature and lifecycle:

  • Planned: Expected future transactions with optional recurrence.
  • Actual: Transactions that have already occurred.
  • One-time: Non-recurring transactions.
  • Recurring: Transactions that repeat on a schedule.

Projections Guide

Understanding Projections

  • Forecast grid: See projected account balances over time.
  • How they work: Start with current balance, add income, subtract expenses.
  • Recalculate: Refresh projections after any change.
  • Scenario comparison: Validate which plan is most sustainable.

Generating Projections

Projections show you where your finances are headed based on your planned transactions and account balances.

  1. Ensure all your accounts and planned transactions are set up correctly.
  2. Click "Generate Projections" in the Forecast view.
  3. Select the projection period (typically 90 days).
  4. Review the projected balances for each account.
  5. Identify any accounts that go negative or reach critical levels.
  6. Adjust transactions or income as needed to improve the forecast.

Interpreting the Projection Grid

The projection grid displays each account as a column and each time period as a row. Each cell shows the projected balance at that point in time.

  • Red values indicate negative balances (overspending).
  • Green values indicate positive balances (healthy savings).
  • Watch for trends: steadily declining or rising balances indicate unsustainable patterns.
  • Compare multiple scenarios to find the most sustainable plan.

Refining Your Projections

Projections are only as good as your data. Here's how to improve accuracy:

  • Keep account balances current (weekly updates).
  • Review and update planned transactions regularly.
  • Use recurrence for all repeating payments.
  • Add periodic changes for salary increases or known expense changes.
  • Compare projections to actuals and adjust the plan accordingly.

Scenario Comparison

Create multiple scenarios with different assumptions to compare outcomes:

  • Baseline: Your expected reality.
  • Conservative: Higher expenses, lower income.
  • Optimistic: Lower expenses, higher income.
  • What-if: Test specific changes like a new job or expense.

Recurrence & Scheduling

Recurrence Patterns

  • One-time: Transactions that happen only once.
  • Weekly: Specify days of the week (e.g., every Monday and Friday).
  • Monthly: Specific day of month (e.g., 15th) or nth weekday (e.g., first Tuesday).
  • Quarterly: Same patterns as monthly, applied every 3 months.
  • Yearly: Specific month and day (e.g., December 25th).

Setting Up Recurrence

Recurrence allows you to define repeating transactions once and have them automatically apply across your projection period.

One-Time Transactions

Select "None" for recurrence. The transaction will occur only on the date you specify.

Weekly Recurrence

Choose the days of the week the transaction occurs. For example:

  • Paycheck: Every Friday
  • Gym visits: Monday, Wednesday, Friday
  • Gas: Every Tuesday

Monthly Recurrence

You have two options for monthly recurrence:

  • Specific day: The 15th of each month (good for mid-month bills).
  • Nth weekday: The first Tuesday, third Friday, last Monday, etc. (useful for variable schedules).

Quarterly Recurrence

Same options as monthly, but transactions repeat every 3 months. Examples:

  • Quarterly tax payments
  • Annual insurance payments (every 3 months)
  • Seasonal expenses

Yearly Recurrence

Specify the month and day. Examples:

  • Annual subscription renewals
  • Holiday bonuses
  • Car registration or license renewal

Best Practices for Recurrence

  • Use recurrence for all repeating payments to reduce data entry.
  • Be specific about days and dates for accuracy.
  • Review recurrence patterns before generating projections.
  • Use periodic changes to handle salary increases or expense adjustments.

Recurrence + Periodic Changes

Combine recurrence with periodic changes to model real-world scenarios like annual raises or inflation-adjusted expenses.

  • Salary: Weekly recurrence + 3% annual increase
  • Rent: Monthly recurrence on the 1st (no change)
  • Utilities: Monthly recurrence + $5 seasonal adjustment

Periodic Changes

Amount Adjustments Over Time

  • Flat increase: Add a fixed amount each period (e.g., +$100 annually).
  • Flat decrease: Subtract a fixed amount each period.
  • Percentage increase: Grow by a percentage (e.g., 3% annually for salary raises).
  • Percentage decrease: Shrink by a percentage.
  • Compound interest: Apply exponential growth (e.g., investment returns).

What are Periodic Changes?

Periodic changes apply adjustments to transaction amounts at regular intervals. Instead of manually changing amounts, you define a change rule that automatically applies each period.

Types of Changes

Flat Increase

Add a fixed dollar amount each period. Example: Salary increases by $100 every quarter.

  • Useful for step raises or bonuses.
  • Good when you know the exact dollar amount of the increase.

Flat Decrease

Subtract a fixed dollar amount each period. Example: Loan principal decreases by $500 monthly.

  • Useful for loan amortization models.
  • Good for tracking declining expenses.

Percentage Increase

Apply a percentage increase each period. Example: Salary grows 3% annually.

  • Useful for inflation adjustments.
  • Good for modeling salary growth or investment returns.
  • Works with any recurrence pattern.

Percentage Decrease

Apply a percentage decrease each period. Example: Expenses shrink 2% annually as you optimize.

  • Useful for modeling cost reduction plans.
  • Good for depreciation calculations.

Compound Interest

Apply exponential growth like investment returns or savings account interest. Example: Investment grows at 7% annually.

  • Useful for investment and savings projections.
  • Accounts for "interest on interest" effects.
  • Best used with accounts rather than individual transactions.

Setting Up a Periodic Change

  1. Create a transaction with recurrence.
  2. Open the recurrence details.
  3. Add a periodic change rule.
  4. Select the change type (flat, percentage, compound).
  5. Enter the amount or percentage.
  6. Specify when changes apply (e.g., annually, every quarter).
  7. Save and generate projections.

Scenario Planning

Using Scenarios Effectively

  • Baseline scenario: Your current reality with existing income and expenses.
  • What-if scenarios: Test changes like salary increases, new expenses, or big purchases.
  • Comparison: Duplicate a scenario to keep the original as a reference.
  • Budget vs General: Budget scenarios are for strict planning; General for open exploration.
  • Date ranges: Set scenario windows to focus on specific time periods.

What is a Scenario?

A scenario is a complete financial plan or "what-if" situation. It contains its own set of accounts, transactions, and projections. You can have multiple scenarios to explore different possibilities without mixing your data.

Scenario Types

Budget Scenarios

A strict financial plan designed to control spending. Budget scenarios include all core sections: Accounts, Transactions (both planned and actual), Budget grid, and Projections.

Key Features:

  • Budget Grid: Define category-level spending limits and track against them
  • Actual Transactions: Record what you actually spend to compare against your plan
  • Variance Tracking: See how your actual spending compares to budgeted amounts
  • Planned Transactions: Model your expected income and expenses

Best for: Monthly budget planning, expense control and monitoring, disciplined financial management.

General Scenarios

An open exploration scenario with more flexibility. General scenarios provide the core planning tools (Accounts, Transactions, Projections) without the strict budget tracking overhead.

Best for: Long-term financial planning, exploratory what-if analysis, modeling without strict budget constraints.

Debt Repayment Scenarios

A specialized scenario type optimized for tracking and modeling debt payoff strategies. Debt Repayment scenarios remove budget tracking and add summary cards that show key payoff metrics at a glance.

Summary Cards

Debt Repayment scenarios feature summary cards displayed at the top of your forecast, showing:

  • Per-Account Cards: Current balance, projected end balance, payoff date, and total interest paid for each debt account
  • Overall Total Card: Aggregated totals across all debt accounts including total current debt, total projected end balance, total interest paid, and account count

Available Sections

Debt Repayment scenarios include:

  • Summary Cards: Quick overview of payoff progress and totals
  • Accounts: Define your debt accounts with balances and interest rates
  • Transactions: Model your payment strategy (planned transactions only - actual transactions are hidden)
  • Projections: Month-by-month payoff schedule showing interest, payments, and remaining balance

Note: Budget grid and Actual Transactions sections are hidden in Debt Repayment scenarios as they're not relevant for debt payoff modeling.

Using Debt Repayment Scenarios

  1. Create the scenario: Select "Debt Repayment" from the scenario type dropdown
  2. Add your debts: In the Accounts section, add each debt with its current balance and interest rate (e.g., "Visa CC: $5,000 @ 18% APR")
  3. Define payments: In the Transactions section, add your planned monthly payments for each debt
  4. Generate projections: Click "Generate Projections" to see your month-by-month payoff schedule
  5. Review summary cards: Check the summary cards to see payoff dates, total interest, and progress
  6. Experiment: Adjust payment amounts to see how increasing payments affects payoff date and total interest paid

Example: Paying Off Two Debts

Setup:

  • Visa CC: $5,000 @ 18% APR
  • Student Loan: $10,000 @ 5% APR
  • Payment Strategy: $250/month to Visa, $100/month to Student Loan

Summary Cards Show:

  • Visa CC Card: Current: $5,000, End: $0, Payoff: March 2027, Interest: $750
  • Student Loan Card: Current: $10,000, End: $0, Payoff: June 2027, Interest: $1,700
  • Overall Total Card: Current: $15,000, End: $0, Total Interest: $2,450, Accounts: 2

You can then experiment: increase the Visa payment to $300 and regenerate projections to see how it reduces both payoff time and total interest.

Glossary

A reference guide to common terms used throughout FTrack.

A

Account
A place where money lives or is owed. Examples: checking account, savings account, credit card, loan. Accounts are fundamental to FTrack's data model.
Account Type
The classification of an account. Types include: Asset, Liability, Income, and Expense. These help organize and categorize your financial information.
Actual Transaction
A transaction that has already occurred in the real world. Used to track real spending and compare against planned amounts.

B

Balance
The amount of money in an account at a specific point in time. Opening balance is your starting point; projected balance shows where you'll be in the future.
Budget
A type of scenario that enforces strict financial planning. Budget scenarios are designed to control spending and monitor expenses.

C

Compound Interest
A periodic change that applies exponential growth, like investment returns. Interest earned also earns interest, creating accelerating growth.

D

Dual-Entry Transaction
A transaction that involves two accounts. Money moves from one account to another, maintaining double-entry accounting principles.

E

Expense
Money that flows out, such as rent, utilities, groceries, or subscriptions. Categorized as an Account Type for organizational purposes.

F

Flat Change
A periodic change that adds or subtracts a fixed dollar amount each period. Example: salary increases by $100 quarterly.
Forecast
See "Projection". The process of predicting future financial balances based on planned transactions and current accounts.

I

Income
Money flowing in, such as salary, bonuses, or interest earned. Categorized as an Account Type for organization.

L

Liability
Money you owe to others. Examples: credit cards, loans, mortgages. Tracked as an Account Type.

O

Opening Balance
The starting balance of an account, as of the current date. This is the basis for all future projections.

P

Percentage Change
A periodic change that applies a percentage adjustment each period. Example: salary grows 3% annually.
Periodic Change
An automatic adjustment to a transaction amount that occurs at regular intervals. Used to model salary raises, inflation, and other gradual changes.
Planned Transaction
An expected future transaction with a set amount and date. Can have recurrence and periodic changes.
Projection
A forecast of future account balances based on current balances and planned transactions. Shows where your finances are headed.

Q

Quarterly
Occurring every 3 months. A recurrence pattern for transactions that repeat 4 times per year.

R

Recurrence
A repeating schedule for transactions. Patterns include weekly, monthly, quarterly, and yearly. Reduces data entry for repeating payments.

S

Scenario
A complete financial plan or "what-if" situation. Contains its own accounts, transactions, and projections. Allows side-by-side comparison of different plans.

T

Transaction
A movement of money between accounts. Can be planned (expected future) or actual (already occurred).

V

Variance
The difference between planned and actual amounts. Used to refine budgets and identify spending patterns.

W

What-If Scenario
A scenario designed to explore a specific change or decision. Example: "What if I get a raise?" or "What if I buy a car?"

Frequently Asked Questions

Q: How do I get started with FTrack?

Start by visiting the Getting Started section in the documentation. In brief: create a scenario, add your accounts with opening balances, add planned transactions for income and expenses, set up recurrence for repeating payments, then generate projections to see your financial forecast.

Q: Can I have multiple scenarios?

Yes! FTrack supports multiple scenarios. Create as many as you need. Each scenario has its own accounts, transactions, and projections. This lets you compare different financial plans side-by-side.

Q: How accurate are projections?

Projections are only as accurate as your data. Keep your account balances current (weekly is recommended), ensure all recurring transactions are set up correctly, and add periodic changes for known adjustments. The more accurate your input, the more reliable your projections.

Q: Can I edit a completed transaction?

Yes. Click on any transaction row to edit it. Changes are automatically saved. You can update amounts, dates, accounts, or any other field. This applies to both planned and actual transactions.

Q: What if I make a mistake?

All data is automatically saved, so you won't lose changes accidentally. If you need to undo a change, simply edit the row back to its previous state. For bulk changes, you can export your data, modify it, and re-import.

Q: How do I handle a one-time expense or income?

Create a transaction and set recurrence to "None" (one-time). This transaction will occur only on the date you specify and won't repeat.

Q: How do I model a salary increase?

Create your paycheck as a planned transaction with weekly or bi-weekly recurrence. Then add a periodic change to your transaction with a percentage increase (e.g., 3% annually) or flat increase (e.g., $100 per quarter). FTrack will automatically adjust future paychecks.

Q: Can I track actual spending alongside my budget?

Yes. FTrack has both Planned Transactions and Actual Transactions. Log your actuals as they happen, and the system will show you variance between what you planned and what actually occurred. This helps refine your budgets over time.

Q: How do I compare two scenarios?

Generate projections for each scenario, then review side-by-side in the Forecast view. You can see which scenario has better cash flow, higher ending balances, or fewer risky periods (when balances go negative).

Q: What's the difference between Budget and General scenarios?

Budget scenarios are designed for strict financial planning and expense control. General scenarios are more flexible and suited for long-term planning and exploratory what-if analysis. Choose based on your use case.

Q: How do I handle a recurring payment with a variable amount?

Create the transaction with recurrence and an average or base amount. Use periodic changes if the amount adjusts predictably (e.g., utility bills with seasonal adjustments). For truly variable amounts, log them as actual transactions after they occur.

Q: Can I export my data?

Yes. Click the "Export Data" button in the navbar. Your entire data set (all scenarios, accounts, transactions) will download as a JSON file. You can back this up or use it to transfer data between devices.

Q: How do I import data from a file?

Click the "Import Data" button in the navbar and select your exported JSON file. FTrack will load the data. Be careful: importing replaces your current data, so export first if you want to preserve anything.

Q: Where is my data stored?

Data is stored locally on your device. In the Electron desktop app, data is stored in the application data directory. In the web version, data is stored in browser local storage. No data is sent to external servers.

Q: Can I delete a scenario?

Yes. From the Forecast view, you can delete scenarios you no longer need. Be careful: deletion is permanent. Consider exporting your data first if you want to preserve a record.

Q: How do I model a loan payment?

Create a recurring transaction from your checking account to your loan liability account with the payment amount. If the loan has interest, model that as a separate transaction, or note it as a separate account. For amortizing loans, use periodic changes to adjust the principal portion over time.

Q: What keyboard shortcuts are available?

See the Keyboard Shortcuts section in the documentation for a complete list. Key ones: Tab to navigate cells, Enter to save, Cmd+Shift+A (Mac) or Ctrl+Shift+A (Windows) to add rows, Delete to remove rows.

Q: How often should I update my data?

For best results, update your account balances weekly and log actual transactions as they occur. This keeps your projections accurate and helps you identify spending patterns. Monthly updates are the minimum.

Q: Can FTrack handle credit cards?

Yes. Credit cards are modeled as Liability accounts. Create an account for each credit card with its opening balance. When you make a purchase, the transaction moves money from the card to an expense account. When you pay it off, move money from checking to the credit card account.

Q: How do I contact support or report a bug?

Check the project's GitHub repository or documentation for support information. For bugs, provide details about what happened, the steps to reproduce, and your data (anonymized if needed).
