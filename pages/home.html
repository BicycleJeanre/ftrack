Getting Started: Create Your First Budget

A budget is your financial roadmap. It helps you plan your income and expenses so you can see if your money will last through the month, and identify where you can save. FTrack makes this simple by letting you forecast multiple scenarios and compare them side-by-side.

What is a Scenario?

A scenario is a financial plan. You might have a baseline scenario showing your current reality, plus scenarios for "if I save $200/month" or "if I get a raise." Each scenario contains the same accounts, but with different transactions and projections.

Step-by-Step: Build Your First Budget

  1. Create a scenario: Click the Forecast button, then create a new scenario named "My Budget" and set the type to Budget.
  2. Add your accounts: List the accounts where your money lives (e.g., Checking, Savings, Credit Card). Enter each account's current balance.
  3. Add your income: Create a Planned Transaction for your paycheck. Set it to recur weekly or monthly depending on your pay schedule.
  4. Add your expenses: Add Planned Transactions for recurring expenses like rent, utilities, groceries, and subscriptions. Use recurrence for monthly or weekly bills.
  5. Configure recurrence: For repeating payments, set them to recur on the correct day each week or month.
  6. Run a projection: Click "Generate Projections" to see your forecasted balances over the next 90 days.
  7. Review and adjust: Look at the projection. If your balance goes negative, adjust your spending or add more income until it looks sustainable.
  8. Save your plan: Your budget is automatically saved. You can now track it and compare it to actual spending.

Pro Tips for Beginners

  • Start simple: Don't worry about every small expense. Focus on the big items first (rent, paycheck, major bills).
  • Keep one baseline: Always have one scenario that matches reality. Use it to compare "what-if" scenarios against.
  • Recurrence is your friend: Most expenses are monthly or weekly. Use recurrence so you don't have to enter each payment manually.
  • Log actuals regularly: Once a week, mark transactions as "actual" to see how your real spending compares to your plan.
  • Adjust as needed: Your budget isn't set in stone. Update it when your situation changes (raise, new expense, etc.).

Accounts Guide

Account Basics

  • What they represent: Real places where your money lives.
  • Balances: Keep opening balances aligned with real accounts.
  • Types: Categorize accounts as Asset, Liability, Income, or Expense.
  • Status: Deactivate closed accounts without deleting history.

Transactions Guide

Planned Transactions

  • Purpose: Define all expected future income and expenses.
  • Recurrence: Set weekly, monthly, or custom repeating schedules.
  • Periodic change: Apply increases, decreases, or compounding effects.
  • Dual-entry: Always assign a secondary account for accuracy.

Actual Transactions

  • Reality check: Mark planned items as completed or adjust amounts.
  • Period filters: Focus on a specific month or time range.
  • Variance: Compare planned vs actual spending and income.

Projections Guide

Understanding Projections

  • Forecast grid: See projected account balances over time.
  • How they work: Start with current balance, add income, subtract expenses.
  • Recalculate: Refresh projections after any change.
  • Scenario comparison: Validate which plan is most sustainable.

Recurrence & Scheduling

Recurrence Patterns

  • One-time: Transactions that happen only once.
  • Weekly: Specify days of the week (e.g., every Monday and Friday).
  • Monthly: Specific day of month (e.g., 15th) or nth weekday (e.g., first Tuesday).
  • Quarterly: Same patterns as monthly, applied every 3 months.
  • Yearly: Specific month and day (e.g., December 25th).

Periodic Changes

Amount Adjustments Over Time

  • Flat increase: Add a fixed amount each period (e.g., +$100 annually).
  • Flat decrease: Subtract a fixed amount each period.
  • Percentage increase: Grow by a percentage (e.g., 3% annually for salary raises).
  • Percentage decrease: Shrink by a percentage.
  • Compound interest: Apply exponential growth (e.g., investment returns).

Scenario Planning

Using Scenarios Effectively

  • Baseline scenario: Your current reality with existing income and expenses.
  • What-if scenarios: Test changes like salary increases, new expenses, or big purchases.
  • Comparison: Duplicate a scenario to keep the original as a reference.
  • Budget vs General: Budget scenarios are for strict planning; General for open exploration.
  • Date ranges: Set scenario windows to focus on specific time periods.

Glossary

Key Terms

  • Account: A place where money lives (checking, savings, credit card).
  • Transaction: A movement of money between accounts.
  • Planned Transaction: An expected future transaction with recurrence.
  • Actual Transaction: A transaction that actually occurred.
  • Projection: A forecast of future account balances.
  • Scenario: A named financial plan or what-if situation.
  • Recurrence: A repeating schedule (weekly, monthly, etc.).
  • Periodic change: An adjustment to a transaction amount over time.
